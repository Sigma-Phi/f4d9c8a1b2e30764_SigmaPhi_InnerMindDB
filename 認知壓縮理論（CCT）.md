Semantic Compression Theory (SCT)

Language as Lossy Compression of Experience Distributions

Abstract

We propose Semantic Compression Theory (SCT), a unified model of language in which lexical items are interpreted as lossy compressions of high-dimensional experiential distributions. Meaning is defined as a latent variable distribution induced by contextual usage, and linguistic disagreement is formalized as divergence between such distributions across agents. We derive measurable quantities for semantic variance, reification loss, and inter-agent semantic divergence, and propose empirical tests using distributional language models and multi-corpus comparisons.

⸻

1. Introduction

Human language is traditionally modeled as a symbolic system grounded in reference. However, recent advances in Distributional Semantics and Machine Learning suggest that meaning is better understood as a statistical property of usage rather than a fixed entity.

We propose that language functions as a lossy compression system over experiential distributions, where meaning arises from the encoding and decoding of latent experiential states.

⸻

2. Related Work

SCT builds on three research streams:

* Information-theoretic views of cognition in Information Theory
* Distributional models of semantics (word embeddings, contextual representations)
* Predictive processing models in Cognitive Science

Unlike prior work, SCT explicitly formalizes semantic disagreement as distributional divergence between latent representations across agents.
